01 — THE PROBLEM
Starting Point

A brand scaling spend without knowing which customers were worth keeping

The brand had grown to $15M in annual revenue on the back of aggressive paid acquisition across Meta and Google. Revenue was increasing — but so was CAC, and the leadership team had a nagging suspicion: too many customers were buying once and never coming back.

📉

Rising Customer Acquisition Cost

CAC had climbed quarter over quarter as the brand scaled spend, with no corresponding improvement in customer retention or LTV to offset it.

🔁

14% 90-Day Repeat Rate

Only 14 in 100 customers made a second purchase within 90 days — well below category benchmarks for jewelry brands at their revenue tier.

📊

Dashboard Blind Spots

Revenue dashboards reported healthy top-line growth but couldn't distinguish between high-LTV customers and one-time buyers — making every acquisition decision a guess.

🗃️

Fragmented Data

Customer data lived in silos: Shopify for transactions, Meta Ads Manager for acquisition, a separate email platform for CRM — none of them connected at the customer level.

The core question leadership couldn't answer: Are we acquiring customers who will come back, or are we paying to fill a leaky bucket? Without connecting acquisition data to long-term purchase behaviour, there was no way to know — and no way to fix it.

04 — THE ANALYSIS
Analytical Process

How we found it — step by step

Four analytical layers were applied sequentially. Each layer answered a specific question and built the foundation for the next.

STEP
01

Data Unification & Customer Identity Resolution

Shopify transaction records, Meta Ads attribution data, Google Ads click data, and email engagement logs were merged at the customer level using email address as the primary key. Each customer record now carried their full history: first acquisition source, first product purchased, subsequent purchase dates, discount usage, and total spend.

What this unlocked: For the first time, leadership could see a single customer's journey — from first ad click to most recent purchase — in one place. This made cohort analysis possible.
STEP
02

LTV Segmentation by First Purchase Category

All customers were segmented by the category of their first purchase. 12-month LTV was then calculated per segment — defined as total revenue per customer from their first purchase date through 12 months. This revealed that first product category was a far stronger predictor of long-term value than acquisition channel, demographics, or discount usage.

The finding: Silver stacking ring buyers had a 12-month LTV of $680 on average. Statement piece buyers averaged $170. Customers starting with charm bracelets were the second-highest LTV group at $410.
STEP
03

Churn Prediction Modelling

A churn prediction model was built using three behavioural signals available for every customer: days since last purchase (recency), total number of visits (frequency), and whether the customer had ever used a discount code. Customers in the top quartile of churn risk were flagged for proactive re-engagement before their window closed.

The outcome: 18% of at-risk customers who received targeted re-engagement sequences made a subsequent purchase within 30 days — customers who would otherwise have lapsed silently.
STEP
04

Acquisition Source Mapping

High-LTV customer profiles were traced back to their acquisition sources to identify which creative angles, audiences, and channels were generating stacking ring buyers. This revealed that certain Meta ad creatives — specifically lifestyle imagery showing ring stacking — were disproportionately attracting the highest-LTV customers, but were being under-funded relative to statement piece creatives.

The action: Budget was reallocated toward the creative angles and audience signals correlated with high-LTV first purchases. CAC initially rose slightly before stabilising — then dropped 23% within 90 days as the algorithm optimised toward higher-quality signals.

07 — APPLICATION
Relevance

Why this matters for omnichannel brands

The same fragmentation problem that made this analysis necessary becomes significantly worse as brands expand across DTC, marketplaces, and retail. Cross-channel growth without unified data creates blind spots that compound over time.

🔗

Cross-channel Customer Identity

When the same customer buys on your site, Amazon, and in a retail partner, those transactions need to be connected. Without it, you're optimising three siloed revenue streams instead of one customer relationship.

📦

Product-LTV Mapping by Channel

The products that perform well on marketplaces aren't always the ones that build long-term loyalty on DTC. Understanding LTV by first product, per channel, changes which SKUs you promote where.

🎯

Acquisition Quality Scoring

Not all paid channels deliver the same quality of customer. An LTV-adjusted view of channel performance often reveals that a higher-CAC channel is actually cheaper per retained customer — a counterintuitive finding that changes budget allocation entirely.

⚡

Early Churn Signals

At scale, churn is nearly invisible until it's too late. Predictive models built on visit frequency, recency, and discount behaviour give brands a 30–60 day window to intervene before a customer is lost.
